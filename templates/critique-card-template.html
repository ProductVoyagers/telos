Model: a daily habit tracker. The "Today" screen lists the day's habits with one-tap check circles, a streak banner at the top, and a bottom tab bar (Today / Habits / Stats / Me).

What Serves the KRs
The streak banner ("5-day streak") is the first thing the user sees.
Placing the streak at the very top, above the habits, makes the thing the user has to lose the most prominent element on screen. Loss aversion is the strongest lever a habit app has for daily return: people come back to protect a streak more reliably than to chase a new reward. Surfacing it immediately is the right call for 7-day retention.

Retention Engagement
One-tap check-off keeps the cost of a daily visit near zero.
Each habit completes with a single tap on the check circle, with no confirmation step or modal. The lower the effort per check-in, the more likely the behavior survives a busy day. For a habit that must repeat daily to build retention, removing friction from the core action directly supports both KRs.

What to Reconsider
1
Nothing on the screen gives the user a reason to come back tomorrow.
The "Today" screen shows the current day's state, but once habits are checked off there is no forward hook: no "your streak continues tomorrow," no preview of tomorrow's plan, no sense of momentum beyond today. Retention is a tomorrow problem, and the screen only speaks to today. A user who finishes their habits has no reason on-screen to return — they're relying entirely on memory or an external reminder.
Today — after all habits checked
HIGH IMPACT Retention
2
A single missed day resets the streak to zero with nothing to soften the loss.
The streak is the only progress signal, which makes it brittle. The day a user misses, "5-day streak" becomes "0" — and the very mechanic that drove them back becomes a reason to quit, because the cost of restarting feels high. The biggest retention cliff in habit apps is the day after the first miss. With no streak-freeze, grace day, or "you've still done 6 of the last 7 days" framing, a single slip reads as total failure.

HIGH IMPACT Retention
3
The streak is the only measure of progress — there's no weekly or longer-horizon signal.
"5-day streak" and "2 / 4 done" both describe a single point in time. There is no view of the week (e.g. "5 of 7 days active") that would let a user who broke a streak still feel they're winning. A second, more forgiving progress signal gives engaged users something to grow even when the streak resets, which is exactly the KR 2 behavior (weekly active streaks) the app is trying to move.

MEDIUM IMPACT Engagement
4
No reminder or notification opt-in appears anywhere in the flow.
Daily return for a habit app is almost entirely driven by a timely reminder, yet the screen never asks for notification permission or lets the user set a habit time. Without it, retention depends on the user remembering to open the app unprompted — the weakest possible mechanism. The reminder ask is the single highest-leverage retention element and it is absent.

5
The late-day "still incomplete" state isn't designed for — there's no nudge when habits are unfinished.
At "2 / 4 done" the screen looks identical whether it's 9am or 9pm. There's no escalation as the day runs out and the streak is at risk — no "2 habits left to keep your streak" prompt. The moment a streak is in danger is the moment a well-timed nudge matters most, and right now that moment passes silently.

Suggestions Tied to the KRs
6
Ask for notification permission during onboarding, tied to the user's chosen habit time.
When the user adds their first habit, let them pick a time and request notifications in the same step, framed as "We'll remind you at 8am." A reminder set to the moment the habit actually happens is the strongest driver of next-day return. This is the most direct lever on 7-day retention available.
Retention Activation
7
Add a streak-freeze / grace day so one miss doesn't reset progress to zero.
Give users a small number of "freezes" that automatically protect a streak on a missed day (earned by consistency). The day after a miss is the biggest churn cliff — softening it keeps users in the loop long enough to rebuild the habit, directly protecting 7-day retention without diluting the streak's motivational pull.
Retention
8
Show a weekly progress signal ("5 of 7 days") alongside the streak.
Add a forgiving, week-level measure next to the current streak. It gives users who slipped a streak something they're still winning at, and it makes "weekly active streaks" (KR 2) a visible goal the user can aim for, not just an internal metric. Pair it with the streak rather than replacing it.
Engagement Retention
9
Add an end-of-day nudge state: "2 habits left to keep your streak."
When habits remain unfinished and the day is running out, escalate the screen (and a notification) to make the streak-at-risk explicit. Tie the message to the streak the user is protecting. This converts the highest-intent moment — streak in danger — into a completed check-in, serving both KRs at once.
